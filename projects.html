Below is an incomplete list of research themes and potential/ongoing projects in our lab. Candidates are welcome to get in touch and discuss these and/or any other potential projects aligned with one or more of our research themes.

Theme: Mapping and modelling trans-regulatory networks

Project 1: Reconstructing trans-regulatory networks by trans-omic data integration

A major initiative in our group is to integrate trans-omics datasets generated by state‐of‐the‐art mass spectrometer (MS) and next generation sequencer (NGS) from various cell systems. The goal is to reconstruct trans-regulatory networks and to understand how different regulatory machineries (e.g. signalling, transcription, and epigenomics) co-operate to define cell states, functions, and fates.

We have previously developed various computational methods to integrate the multi-layered trans‐omics datasets generated during naive to formative pluripotency transition in ESCs (Yang et al. 2019). Our current research project aims to further this study by developing methods to characterise signaling cascades, transcriptional networks, and translational protein networks and their cross‐talks. This project will provide a unique opportunity for developing computational methods for comprehensive understanding of cell systems and their decision-making process.

Project 2: Understanding the formation of fat tissue

The understanding of fat tissue formation is critical for treating various metabolic and obesity related diseases. In collaboration with Prof. David James lab, we have developed several machine learning approaches to elucidate the signaling cascades in fat cells (Yang et al. 2016; Yang et al. 2015; Humphrey et al. 2013). We have now also profiled the fat cell (3T3-L1) differentiation at matched time points on multiple omic levels to understand the trans-regulatory networks underlying this process. You will get involved in developing key statistical models to integrate trans-omics data and infer their feedback regulation during adipogenesis, the maturation process of fat cells.

Project 3: Stem/progenitor cell differentiation and iPSC reprogramming

The ability of stem/progenitor to self-renew and differentiate into specialised cell types in the body makes them the key cellular tools for stem cell-based therapy, whereby any specific cell populations, tissues, and organs compromised by disease, injury or ageing can be replaced and replenished indefinitely. While tremendous progress has been made in stem cell therapy, attempts to direct stem/progenitor cells to clinically useful cell types have often been fraught with imprecise and incomplete differentiation, a common cause of tumorigenesis, owing to a lack of comprehensive understanding of trans-regulatory networks that underline cell identities and cell-fate decisions.

Leveraging on the recent advances in mass spectrometry (MS) and next-generation sequencing (NGS), this project aims for experimentally profiling the trans-regulatory networks stem/progenitor using trans-omics. Complementary to this, we will also profile the trans-regulatory networks during somatic cell reprogramming to induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs). Generation of these data will allow us to model and subsequently modulate trans-regulatory networks for highly precise stem/progenitor differentiation for use in stem cell-based therapy.

Theme: Single-cell biology and omics

Project 1: Multiple single-cell RNA-seq data integration

We are working on multiple methodological development as well as lab experiment projects on using single-cell omic technologies to understand complex biological systems. One key research direction is the integration of multiple single-cell RNA-seq datasets. To this end, we have developed a computational method together with Prof. Jean Yang's group for multiple single-cell RNA-seq data integration (Lin et al. 2019).

We are now extending this method for ultra-large scale scRNA-seq data integration and for using diverse gene characteristics. Working on this project, you will be exposed to various state-of-the-art statistical methods and their application to the latest biotechnology of single-cell omics.

Project 2: Cell type identification using statistical learning

Cell type identification is a key step in scRNA-seq experiments. We are in process of developing better unsupervised, semi-supervised, and fully supervised learning methods for cell type identification from multiple scRNA-seq datasets.

One interesting finding so far is that not all 'similarity' metrics are equally useful in analysing scRNA-seq data (Kim et al. 2018). Note that most of the clustering and classification methods utilise some kind of similarity metric to compare cells. Therefore, the finding has great implication on both the development of computational methods for scNRA-seq data analysis and the biology of individual cells. This project is computational and requires a good grasp of machine learning techniques. Candidates also have opportunity to explore deep learning methods and their application in this domain.

Project 3: Single-cell profiling of ESC differentiation to multiple lineages

This project involves experimental profiling of single cells in ESC population and during their differentiation to multiple cell lineages. The 10x genomics platform at CMRI is ideal for this project and accessible to our lab. Together with Prof. Patrick Tam's lab, we have already established single-cell sequencing and epigenomic sequencing (e.g. ATAC-seq) platforms at CMRI.

The candidate will be uniquely positioned to understand ESC differentiation and early embryogenesis. Such knowledge will be instrumental in developing stem cell-based therapy.

Theme: Computational methodology innovation in bioinformatics

Project 1: Machine learning application in trans-omics

Build on top of mapping and modelling trans-omic networks, a PhD position is available in developing and applying machine learning models to multi-layered trans‐omics datasets generated by MS-based phospho(proteomics) and NGS-based sequencing.

Our research project, funded by the Australian Research Council (ARC), aims to develop novel machine learning algorithms to analyse and integrate large‐scale MS‐based omics data with ultra‐fast NGS‐based omics data generated from complex biological systems. Characterising the signaling cascades, transcriptional networks, and translational protein networks and their cross‐talks are critical for comprehensive understanding of complex biological systems. Our large‐scale multi-layered trans‐omics data generated from state‐of‐the‐art technological platforms provide a unique opportunity to uncover novel biology and molecular mechanisms that are critical for treating complex diseases and personalised medicine.

Project 2: Application of deep learning in bioinformatics

Deep learning is the latest development in machine learning that has been successfully utilised to address many bioinformatics applications. We are interested in its application such as in transcription factor (TF) binding site identification and target gene prediction.

The advance in chromatin immunoprecipitation followed by ultrafast sequencing (ChIP-seq) allows the profiling of TF binding sites genome-wide in a population of cells. The massive amount of sequencing data generated from these genome-wide profiling of TF requires sophisticated computational algorithms to be developed for accurately identifying TF binding sites. In this project, we aim to develop and apply deep learning models for predicting TF binding sites by integrating ChIP-seq data with other biological knowledge. This project aims to develop and apply cutting-edge deep learning algorithms for solving a key biological problem. You will get involved in all aspects of the development including algorithm design, implementation and testing.

Project 3: Application of semi-supervised learning in bioinformatics

Identification of transcription factor target genes using machine learning We have recently developed several semi-supervised and/or knowledge-based learning methods for learning with only positive examples and in the presence of data noise (e.g. Yang et al. 2018). We are now extending semi-supervised learning methods for various bioinformatics application. This includes the identification of transcription factor target genes in differentiating mouse embryonic stem cells (mESCs) by integrating transcriptomics, proteomics, and epigenomics data; the classification of cell types from single-cell RNA-seq data.

Candidates involved in this project we learn state-of-the-art machine learning methods and their applications to cutting-edge systems biology.

Project 4: Pathway reconstruction and annotation using statistical models and trans-omics data sets

While all cells from a given organism have the same DNA sequence that codes for the same genes, different cell types of that organism only have a subset of genes “turned on”. Genes are commonly annotated into pathways for summarising their collective effect in the biological systems. One of the main drawbacks in current pathway annotation is that they are NOT cell type-specific.

We propose to identify and curate cell type-specific pathways, for example, in embryonic stem cells (ESCs) using the trans-omics datasets. The key assumption is that genes within a pathway should have correlated expression profile changes when perturbed. We have collected ESC differentiation data profiled in a time-course on both proteome and transcriptome levels. Following the above assumption, we aim to (1) identify pathways that are regulated specifically in ESC differentiation; and (2) curate ESC-specific pathways using statistical learning. This project will expose you to the development and application of cutting-edge statistical learning methods to the state-of-the-art bio-molecular applications. It sits at the heart of the interdisciplinary research conducted in our group.
